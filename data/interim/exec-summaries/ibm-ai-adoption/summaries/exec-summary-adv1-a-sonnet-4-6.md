# Executive Summary - Enterprise AI Adoption (adv1-a, Sonnet 4.6)

It appears that enterprise adoption of artificial intelligence may be experiencing a period of broadly sustained growth, though the picture that emerges from the research is arguably a mixed one. A significant share of large organisations — those with what might be considered a substantial employee base — were found to have actively deployed AI in some form, while a comparably large proportion appear to remain at earlier stages of exploration or experimentation, seemingly held back by a range of persistent challenges.

It was found that among those organisations already engaging with AI in some capacity, a majority could be said to have broadly accelerated their investment or rollout activity over what appears to have been roughly a two-year period, suggesting that early experience with the technology may be encouraging further commitment. This pattern arguably points to a dynamic in which a relatively small group of earlier movers could be driving much of the apparent momentum in overall adoption figures.

The research seems to indicate that a wide range of countries contributed to the findings, with some markets appearing to show notably higher rates of active deployment than others, though the reasons for these differences may be complex and difficult to attribute with certainty.

Among the factors that appear to be driving adoption, advances that may have made AI tools more accessible, alongside pressure to reduce costs and automate certain processes, were identified as potentially important influences. At the same time, it was found that barriers including limited relevant skills, data complexity, and ethical concerns could be inhibiting a significant portion of organisations from moving beyond early-stage activity.

The findings may suggest that addressing the skills gap and improving data readiness would arguably be among the more impactful steps organisations could take to broaden meaningful deployment.
